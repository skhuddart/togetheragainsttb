About Tuberculosis (TB)

What is TB?

Tuberculosis (TB) is an infectious disease caused by the bacterium Mycobacterium tuberculosis. While TB most commonly affects the lungs, it can affect nearly any part of the body, including the kidneys, spine, and brain.

TB is preventable and curable — but without treatment, it can be life-threatening. Globally, TB is one of the leading infectious disease killers, causing approximately 1.3 million deaths in 2022. TB is also the leading cause of death among people living with HIV.

TB is curable. With the right treatment and support, people with TB can and do recover fully. Organizations like Together Against TB exist to ensure that everyone has what they need to complete treatment.

TB Infection vs. TB Disease

Not everyone infected with TB bacteria becomes sick. There are two distinct stages.

Latent TB Infection (LTBI)

  • TB bacteria are present in the body but inactive (dormant)
  • No symptoms — you feel completely healthy
  • Cannot spread TB to others
  • About 1 in 4 people worldwide has latent TB; over 2 million Californians are estimated to have LTBI
  • Without treatment, about 5–10% will develop active TB disease at some point in their life
  • Treatment for LTBI can prevent progression to active disease and is one of the most effective ways to reduce TB cases

Active TB Disease

  • TB bacteria are actively multiplying and causing illness
  • Symptoms are present and can worsen over time
  • Can spread TB to others through the air
  • Requires prompt medical treatment with antibiotics
  • Treatment typically lasts 6 months
  • Most people recover fully with proper treatment

How TB Spreads

TB spreads through the air when a person with active pulmonary TB coughs, sneezes, speaks, or sings. Tiny infectious droplets can linger in the air for hours. Prolonged, close contact is typically needed for transmission.

TB is NOT spread by:

  • Shaking hands or hugging
  • Sharing food or drinks
  • Kissing
  • Touching bed linens or toilet seats
  • Contact with surfaces or objects

Signs & Symptoms

Symptoms of active TB disease include:

  • Persistent cough lasting 3 weeks or longer
  • Coughing up blood or sputum
  • Chest pain
  • Fatigue and weakness
  • Night sweats
  • Fever and chills
  • Loss of appetite
  • Unexplained weight loss

Symptoms may be mild at first and gradually worsen. TB outside the lungs may cause symptoms in other parts of the body.

Testing & Diagnosis

Testing is recommended for people at higher risk, including those who have been in close contact with someone with active TB, people living with HIV, and people born in countries with high rates of TB.

  • TB skin test (TST) or TB blood test (IGRA): Checks whether you have been infected with TB bacteria
  • Chest X-ray: Looks for signs of TB disease in the lungs
  • Sputum culture: Confirms active TB and tests for drug resistance

Treatment

TB is curable with antibiotics. It is essential to take all medications as prescribed for the full duration — stopping early can lead to drug resistance.

  • Standard treatment: 4 antibiotics for 2 months, then 2 antibiotics for 4 more months (6 months total)
  • Drug-resistant TB (MDR-TB) requires longer treatment with second-line medications
  • Directly Observed Therapy (DOT): A healthcare worker observes medication doses to support completion and prevent resistance
  • Latent TB is also treatable with shorter regimens to prevent it from becoming active

The Challenges of TB Treatment

TB treatment is long, demanding, and affects every part of a person's life — not just their health.

Medication Side Effects

First-line TB antibiotics can cause nausea, liver toxicity, peripheral neuropathy, vision changes, joint pain, and skin rashes. These side effects are serious and require regular medical monitoring. For drug-resistant TB, second-line medications carry even harsher effects — including hearing loss — making adherence over many months genuinely difficult.

Medical Isolation

People with active TB must isolate — often for several weeks — until they are no longer infectious. This means no work, no school, and limited contact with family. Isolation can be legally mandated, and it cuts people off from income, food, and support at exactly the moment they need it most.

Stigma & Mental Health

TB carries a deep social stigma tied to its history of poverty and contagion. Fear of judgment leads people to delay testing, hide diagnoses, and self-isolate beyond what is medically necessary. Anxiety, depression, and loneliness are common during months of treatment — and stigma is especially harmful in the communities most affected by TB.

Financial & Social Hardship

Weeks of isolation and frequent clinic visits mean lost wages, risk of job loss, and difficulty paying rent. Many patients are hourly workers or undocumented immigrants with little safety net. Transportation, food, and basic essentials become hard to access — turning a medical diagnosis into an economic crisis.

This is why Together Against TB exists. We provide care packages, emergency financial assistance, and community support to help people get through the full course of treatment — because finishing treatment isn't just a medical challenge, it's a social and economic one too.

Who is most affected?

TB in California disproportionately affects immigrant communities, people experiencing homelessness, people living with HIV, and those with limited access to healthcare. In 2025, approximately 47% of cases occurred in non-Hispanic Asian persons and 43% in Hispanic persons. In every racial and ethnic group, TB rates were higher among foreign-born individuals than those born in the U.S.

The hidden burden: Latent TB

More than 2 million Californians are estimated to have latent TB infection — but only about 23% are aware of it, and only 13% have been treated. Since statewide reporting began in 2019, over 3.4 million people have been tested and more than 408,000 have tested positive. Treating latent TB is one of the most effective ways to prevent future active disease.

Outbreaks and children

In 2025, there were 6 new TB outbreaks and 9 ongoing outbreaks across California. That same year, 35 children under five years old were diagnosed with TB — an increase from the 23–27 cases per year seen in the five preceding years.

The California Department of Public Health (CDPH) TB Control Branch works with local health departments across the state to diagnose, treat, and prevent TB. California is also a national leader in TB advocacy — including through the Coalition for a TB-Free California. Data source: CDPH TB in California 2025 Snapshot.

TB in San Francisco

San Francisco consistently has one of the higher TB rates among California counties. The city's dense urban environment and high proportion of foreign-born residents contribute to this elevated burden.

TB in San Francisco disproportionately affects immigrant communities, people experiencing homelessness, and people living with HIV. Many individuals undergoing TB treatment face additional challenges — including housing instability, lost wages during isolation, and the difficulty of completing a 6-month medication regimen without support.

No one should face TB treatment alone. Together Against TB was founded because we saw our neighbors in San Francisco facing impossible choices during treatment — between rent and groceries, between medical appointments and other essentials. We're here to help bridge that gap.

Resources & Getting Help

If you think you or someone you know may have TB, contact a healthcare provider or your local health department right away. TB testing and treatment are free through public health programs in California.
